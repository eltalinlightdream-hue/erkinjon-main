How Does Watching Sport Influence the Brain?

A When we watch a sporting event, we are not passive observers. Research has shown that the brains of spectators are highly active, processing the movements of athletes in ways that go far beyond simple visual recognition. The neural systems that become engaged when we watch sport are fundamentally the same as those used when we actually perform athletic actions ourselves. Understanding why this happens has become a significant area of scientific inquiry.

B At the heart of this phenomenon are cells in the brain known as mirror neurons. These were first discovered in the 1990s when Italian scientists studying macaque monkeys found that certain neurons in the premotor cortex fired both when a monkey performed an action and when it observed another individual performing the same action. The discovery prompted researchers to look for equivalent systems in humans, and subsequent studies using brain imaging technology confirmed that similar mechanisms exist in the human brain.

C Mirror neurons are thought to play a central role in our ability to understand and imitate the actions of others. When we see an athlete execute a particular movement — a tennis serve, a football kick, a gymnastic routine — the mirror neuron system simulates that movement internally. This internal simulation is so detailed that it even captures the intention behind the action, not just the physical motion itself. Experts in a given sport show stronger mirror neuron activation when watching their sport compared to novices, suggesting that the system is refined through experience and practice.

D Beyond mirror neurons, watching sport also activates the motor system more broadly. Studies using transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) have shown that when people observe goal-directed movements, the motor cortex becomes activated in patterns that mirror those observed in performers. This suggests that spectators are, in effect, performing a kind of mental rehearsal of the movements they observe. The degree of motor activation is greater when the observed movements are goal-oriented rather than arbitrary, indicating that the brain is particularly sensitive to purposeful action.

E Another brain region implicated in the experience of watching sport is the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC). This area is associated with the processing of pain and discomfort, and it becomes activated when we watch athletes sustain injuries or exert extreme physical effort. Neuroimaging studies have demonstrated that observing someone in pain activates the same regions of the ACC that are active when we experience pain ourselves. This neural overlap is thought to underpin the empathic responses that spectators feel when watching sport — the wincing, gasping, or shared relief that fans commonly experience.

F The relationship between expertise and neural response in sport spectatorship is particularly striking. When experienced athletes watch others perform in their discipline, there is greater activation in motor and premotor areas compared to when non-athletes watch the same footage. This enhanced response reflects the richer internal representations that trained athletes possess. Their brains have been shaped by years of practice, and this shapes how they perceive and mentally process the movements of others in their sport. This suggests that watching sport could itself be a form of mental training — one that complements physical practice by reinforcing neural pathways associated with skilled movement.
