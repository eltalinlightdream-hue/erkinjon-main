What is the Secret of a Long Life?

The UK has recently exceeded the number of retired pensioners versus under-18s for the first time. Centenarians – people who have reached their hundredth birthday – are the fastest-growing demographic. The number of centenarians in the UK increased 60% since the early 20th century, and by 2030 there will be about 1 million worldwide. People breaking through the 90-year barrier represent a physical elite – their extra years tend to be healthy ones. Supercentenarians, those who have lived to 110 or beyond, show even better patterns of ageing than their younger counterparts.

Kåre Christensen from Denmark studied 3,600 people born in 1905, and found that around one-third remained independent throughout. Of the 166 still alive in 2005, a third were still self-sufficient. Carol Brayne from Cambridge studied 958 people over 90 and found that only one-quarter were living in accommodation specifically designed for older people. Research from China found that centenarians and nonagenarians spend fewer days ill and in bed than younger elderly groups. The picture is clear: the oldest people aren’t necessarily the most frail.

Jessica Even from Ohio State examined more than 400 centenarians and found three distinct categories of extreme longevity. Some 40% were ‘delayers’, who avoided chronic diseases until after the age of 80. Another 40% were ‘survivors’, who suffered chronic diseases before 80 but lived longer than expected. The remaining 20% she called ‘escapers’ – people who reached a century with no sign of common chronic diseases. Notably, one-third of the escapers were male, compared with only 15% of the rest.

Certain geographical regions are remarkable for the longevity of their populations. The Japanese island of Okinawa has 58 centenarians per 100,000 people – the highest proportion in the world. Sardinia and Iceland also have high rates of centenarians. Gerontologists emphasise that regular exercise is a key factor for those wishing to reach old age. Social factors such as wealth influence lifespan, but their importance decreases as people get older. After age 60, genes become the key driver. A twin study by Christensen showed that identical and non-identical twins have largely independent chances of reaching a given age, hinting at genes as a primary influence. Scientists have found over 70 longevity genes in worms, but few have been consistently verified across multiple human populations.

Notes: Centenarian: someone who is 100 years or older. Nonagenarian: someone between 90–99 years old. Gerontologist: a medical professional specialising in ageing.
